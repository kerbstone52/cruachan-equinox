The Cruachan Equinox

The premise:

The ancient Irish have been aware that by being present at particular locations at specific times, they will have observable and repeatable astronomic events (phenomena) visible at other prominent locations on the skyline. That the ancient Irish afforded special significance to these locations where they could observe, on divisional days within the solar and lunar cycles, the sun or moon rise from, or set into, what they regarded as sacred mountains or sacred spaces.

In this document I highlight a series of potential inter-site astronomic relationships, focusing on the Cruachan area and relating to the equinox.

Specifically in the Cruachan context:

Rathcroghan Mound 1 has been positioned to observe the equinox sun rise from Corn Hill 2.

Rathra Ceremonial Enclosure 3 has been positioned to observe the equinox sun set into Croagh Patrick 4.

Castleteheen Bowl Barrow 7 has been positioned to observe the equinox sun rise out of Carn Lamha 6.

Castleteheen Bowl Barrow 7 has been positioned to observe the equinox sun set into Croagh Patrick 4.

Lisheen Bowl Barrow 8 has been positioned to observe the equinox sun rise out of Carn Fraech 5.

Lisheen Bowl Barrow 8 has been positioned to observe the equinox sun set into Croagh Patrick 4.

Carn Lamha 6 has been positioned to observe the equinox sun set into Lisheen Bowl Barrow 8.

Locations

Rathcroghan Mound 1

Rathcroghan is identified as the site of Cruachan, the traditional capital of the Connachta, the prehistoric and early historic rulers of the western territory.

The focal point of the complex, Rathcroghan mound is a broad, flat-topped circular mound with an average diameter of 89 m at the base and a height of 5.5 m. Surveying has revealed that Rathcroghan mound was built on top of an existing monument that was made of two concentric stone built ring banks. A huge enclosure measuring 360 m in diameter and 5 m in depth was also discovered during surveying. This enclosure surrounds the great mound and various other monuments, facilitating comparisons with other royal sites in Ireland such as Tara, Emain Macha and D√∫n Ailinne, which have similar enclosures.

Cruachan features prominently in the mythological Ulster Cycle and it was the home of one of its chief characters Queen Medb.

Locations

Corn Hill 2

Corn Hill, also called Carn Hill or Carn Clonhugh (Irish: Carn Clainne Aodha or Sliabh Cairbr√©), is a hill in County Longford, Republic of Ireland. At 278 metres above sea level, it is the highest hill in the county. The hill is particularly important for the two possible passage grave structures atop it. The largest example is Cairn A (or Carn Caille), marking the highest physical point of the hill with a diameter of 18 m and height of 3 m.

Cairn A (or Carn Caille) has a legend which associates the location as the burial place of Queen Medb's nephew and murderer, Furbaide Ferbend. A second legend attributes its creation by the Cailleach a Bheara or ancient Hag/Witch who dropped stones out of her apron as she flew over the hill. This legend is also associated with many Passage grave sites throughout the island of Ireland.

The regard in which Corn Hill was anciently held, is attested to by the Sheegeeragh passage tombs (approx 8 Kilometers sw from Rathcroghan mound) which have passages specifically directed at Corn Hill.

Rathra 3

Rathra is a multivallate ceremonial enclosure, near Castlerea, County Roscommon, Ireland. This is one of only a handful of quadri-vallate (four banks) enclosures in the whole of Ireland and probably the most spectacular. The Rathra Enclosure lies near the archaeological monuments known as the Rathcroghan Complex and this enclosure is sometimes considered to be part of this group.

The enclosure contains a bowl barrow and a mound barrow which originally had a standing stone on it's summit.

The site sits just off the shoulder of Mewlaghadooey Hill, the summit of which is c. 700m to the ESE and is occupied by a hillfort containing a mound which may be a passage tomb.

Croagh Patrick 4

Croagh Patrick (Irish: Cruach Ph√°draig, meaning "(Saint) Patrick's Stack") and nicknamed the Reek, is a 764 m mountain and an important site of pilgrimage in Mayo, Ireland.

On the last Sunday in July, thousands of pilgrims climb Croagh Patrick in honour of one of Irelands patron saints - Saint Patrick. Some pilgrims carry out 'rounding rituals', in which they pray while walking sunwise around features on the mountain. This activity is regarded by many as a continuation of the pagan Lughnasa festival, the start of the harvest season. St. Patrick's day is celebrated at the spring equinox.

The mountain may have held a pre-christian association with Crom Cruach, a pagan deity.

Carn Fraech 5

Carnfree - Carnfraech is a slightly dished, circular grass covered cairn. The mound is located within a ceremonial enclosure at the highest point of the broad Ard Caoin ridge.

The mound is the reputed burial place of Fraoch. Fraoch is a Connacht hero (and semi-divine as the son of goddess B√©binn). He is the nephew of Boann, goddess of the river Boyne, and son of Idath of the men of Connaught. Fraoch is a contemporary of Queen Maeve and he features prominently in the Ulster Cycle of Irish mythology. The site is also known as Cnoc na Dala (Hill of the Assembly), and it is recorded as the the inauguration site of the O'Conors, the kings of Connacht.

The site is recorded as having been visited by St. Patrick.

Carn Lamha 6

This bowl barrow (RO028-070----) is located on a slight rise, and second highest point of the broad WNW-ESE ridge of Carnfree (Ard Caoin). It is a circular, conical and grass-covered mound surrounded by a slight berm and fosse. Remote sensing surveys at the site and the presence of some visible stone at the NNW side suggest this monument may have a complex internal structure. The mound is located 285m to the east of Carnfree and is surrounded by three smaller degraded ring barrows, an embanked enclosure and two associated pillar stones.

The mound has been re-identified as Carn Lamha - The Mound of the Hand (FitzPatrick 2004, 65). Carn Lamha is recorded in medieval literature as being associated with the warrior Fraoch, and it is within Carn Lamha that one of Fraoch's severed hands is reputed to be buried.

An alternative legend associates the mound as the burial place of the hand of a pupil of St. Patrick.

Castleteheen Bowl Barrow 7

This bowl barrow (RO027-014002-) is located on a slight N-S ridge at the summit of Mewlaghadooey Hill. It compromises a circular, grass-covered flat-topped earthen mound with a slight pit on the summit. It is within the hillfort (RO027-014001-), SW of the centre. Mewlaghadooey (Mullach an D√∫igh) can likely be translated as the inky or dark hilltop. There is no mythology or legends associated with the mound or hill.

Given its prominent position and its size, it is suggested that the mound may contain a passage tomb (Halpin, Newman 2006).

Lisheen Bowl Barrow 8

This bowl barrow (RO027-031----) is situated on the summit of a NW-SE ridge. It has outstanding views in all directions. It is comprised of a circular grass-covered mound with a slight hollow at the summit, and is defined by a shallow fosse which is separated by a berm from an outer bank. There is no identifiable original entrance. There is no mythology or legends associated with the mound. The ridge on which the barrow sits is the furthest visible point eastwards from Croagh Patrick.

The townland of Lisheen translates as 'small stone fort'.

Summary
Context

The essential premise I am exploring is whether the ancient Irish have been cognizant of, and are deliberately observing, inter-site astronomic relationships. This has certainly been the case within the passage tomb and standing stone traditions. Frank Prendergast has demonstrated that certain passage tombs have passages which can be described as directed at other cairns, and other passage tombs which can be described as directed at another cairn and are ALSO aligned on a divisional day horizon event.

In my examination I am seeking to determine and demonstrate where similar relationships might be said to exist not just with passage tombs, but also with other monument types such as henges, barrows etc.

Rush Passage Tomb - an example of a directed and aligned cairn.

Summary
The Equinox

An equinox is commonly regarded as the instant of time when the plane (extended indefinitely in all directions) of Earth's equator passes through the geometric center of the Sun's disk. In simpler terms, those days comprising equal night and day. This occurs twice each year, around 20 March and 23 September. It is certain this has been observed since neolithic times, with many passage tombs aligned for the equinox.

In this presentation, for demonstrative purposes, I have very deliberately selected a series of potential equinox inter-site relationships, as these can still be observed and photographed today. For the other divisional day, and lunar extremes, the sun/moon rise/set positions on the horizon are changing over time. These are no longer observable but are identifiable using computer models.

Shrough Passage Tomb - an example of a directed and equinox aligned cairn.

Summary
The Sp√©ire Project

The examples given in this presentation are a very small extract from a comprehensive examination of hundreds of Irish prehistoric archaeological sites, and locations of significance in Irish mythology. I call this work the Sp√©ire project. As detailed in the introductory premise for this presentation, the objective is to explore the potential that the ancient Irish afforded special significance to locations where they could observe, on specific divisional days of the year, the sun rise from, or set into, what they regarded as sacred mountains. If the premise was correct, it stands to reason very many of these relationships should exist, and be readily identifiable. It is my intention to demonstrate and chart these inter-relationships.
